## Background
The Tay Cities region comprises the 4 local authorities (LA) of Angus, Dundee, Perth & Kinross, and the northeast part of Fife. Lifelong learning (LLL) is the continuous and self-motivated pursuit of education to develop skills beyond required formal education, both for personal growth and/or professional interest. LLL in data, technology, and AI-related skills is increasingly essential for Scotland’s economic growth, including its technological innovation and overall employability of its workforce. However, in recent years, Scotland has seen a significant reduction in publicly funded routes that allow adults, especially disadvantaged groups, to upskill or retain flexibility.
The WEF Future of Jobs Report (2025) highlights that AI and big data, networks and cybersecurity, and technological literacy are anticipated to be the top three fastest growing, in-demand skills in the next five years. The report also notes that employers expect 39% of workers core skills to change by 2030.
At the national policy level, The Scottish Government’s Fit for the Future (2023a) report stresses that post-school learning is “critical national infrastructure” that must be reshaped to fuel Scotland’s economic transformation into a more accessible system for both learners and employers.

## Code Division
Code Division is a Scottish social enterprise that designs and delivers online, flexible digital, and data skills programmes aimed at learners who are underrepresented in the technology workforce, including:
- women
- learners from low-income households
- refugees
- neurodiverse and disabled learners
- career changers


## Purpose of the Project
As learners in the Women and Future Skills programme ran by Code Division, we have been asked to investigate data and reports to understand how LLL funding has changed, how participation and digital skills pathways are being affected, how reductions have impacted disadvantaged groups who are more reliant on flexible learning, and the growing gap between skills demand and training availability.
The context and insights discovered will be used to create evidence-based visuals to support a formal report to the Scottish Government which highlight ongoing issues in Scotland’s digital skills ecosystem, particularly in the Tay Cities region.

## Evidence of the Decline of Lifelong Learning Provisions
### Funding Withdrawls
The following funding initiatives represented the core of Scotland’s adult re-training architecture for digital skills. Their reduction or removal has significantly weakened Scotland’s LLL ecosystem – particularly in digital literacy, reskilling, and mid-career transitions. Digital upskilling courses are often costly and inflexible, meaning that without publicly funded programmes, many adults face substantial barriers to participation.
#### Women Returner Programmes
These programmes were short, funded upskilling initiatives that helped women re-enter work after a career break, often with a focus of digital skills, data, tech and STEM. Code Division ran 8 successful programmes including the Digital Empowerment for Ukrainians Refugee Programme. Funding largely came from the Scottish Government’s Women Returners Fund which launched in 2017 (The Scottish Government, 2019), though funding stopped in 2022 and there are no announcements or Skills Development Scotland (SDS) contract notices for 2023/24 or 2024/25.
This may have contributed to the gendered decline in adult LLL participation for women – especially mothers, carers, and older adults who are disproportionately reliant on flexible and short learning routes.
#### Individual Training Accounts (ITAs)
ITAs support eligible participants with up to £200 per year to access training to gain an industry recognised certificate. Funding was provided by SDS on behalf of the Scottish Government, though SDS confirmed no budget for 2024/25. The evaluation found that ITA funding limited learner choice with almost half of participants having selected courses purely because they fell within the £200 cap, and around half say they would have chosen a different course if more funding had been available (The Scottish Government, 2023b).
#### Digital Start Fund (DSF) and Digital Skills Pipeline (DSP)
Funded by the Scottish government and SDS from 2019-2021, the DSF provided grants to low-income learner to access digital skills training. SDS was provided with £3,083,383 for the DSF programme, which supported 468 learners – 380 of whom successfully completed training.
SDS was provided with £833,225 for the DPS programme, which supported 463 candidates –506 of whom successfully completed training (The Scottish Government, 2023c).
#### The Flexible Workforce Development Fund (FWDF)
The FWDF enables employers to address priority skill gaps by accessing funding to create training programmes that meet their needs. Following the recent Scottish Budget 2024 to 2025 announcement, the Scottish Funding Council (2025) confirmed no funding available for FWDF in academic years 2023-24 and 2024-25.
The Tay Cities Regional Action Plan (2025) directly relates the sharp drop in enrolments at Dundee and Angus College (15,907 in 2023/24 from 24,147 in 2022/23) to the removal of the FWDF.

### Participation
The Tay Cities Regional Skills Action Plan 2025-2029 summarises that Further Education (FE) and Higher Education (HE) have declined – there were 14,990 enrolments at the University of Dundee in 2023/24, a decrease from 17,365 in 2022/23. For Fife College, in 2022/23, there were 29,059 enrolments, compared to 24,780 enrolments in 2023/24.
Modern Apprenticeship (MA) starts decreased across Tayside constituent LA of Angus, Perth and Kinross, and Dundee City in 2024/25 – a decrease from the previous year of 2,004 to 1,944 and fell slightly in Fife at 1,795 in 2024/25 compared to 1,835 in 2023/24.

### Teacher Supply
In the Scottish Technology Ecosystem Review, Logan (2020) stressed that systematic fixes are needed to ensure that earlier and stronger Computing Science (CS) materials are taught in schools. CS is not treated with the same respect as subjects such as Mathematics or Physics, which has resulted in the number of skilled CS teachers in our schools falling. In fact, 17% of our secondary schools have no dedicated CS teachers and rely on co-opting non-specialists to teach CS, which has likely made the syllabus less engaging and contributed to lower progression into HE and beyond. Between 2008 and 2018, the number of teachers whose main subject is CS had fallen by almost 23% (National Records of Scotland, 2020).

### Gender Imbalance
The Scottish Technology Ecosystem Review (2020) also states that on average, 84% of students studying higher CS are male in any given year, which captures a persistent gender imbalance in the school pipeline.
This gender imbalance starts early as The Royal Society of Edinburgh’s Tapping All Talents report (2024) explains, girls are less likely to select CS in early education, meaning the gender imbalance is evident before formal CS teaching begins. This may be attributed to the lack of informal digital opportunities, fewer role models, and cultural/societal perceptions which remain prevalent and expatiate attitudes that insist “computing is for boys”. Some possible ways to address this imbalance may include mandating equal exposure in P5-S1 before choice points, and introducing CS through creative, interdisciplinary learning such as game design, robotics, and animation.

## Data Quality
In Table 3, Perth college appears to have only 20 HE students because almost all of it’s HE provision is delivered through the University of Highlands and Islands framework, this means that college-level HE datasets substantially under-report Perth college’s role in HE and LLL.
The University of Highlands and Islands (UHI) is included as an institution with a physical campus in Perth, although student counts represent the entire university network across the Highlands, Islands, Moray, and Perth. Neither Higher Education Statistics Agency (HESA) nor Scottish Funding Council (SFC) publish a breakdown of student counts by college location. This institution was not included in the visualisations, only for data exploration.
Qualifier figures in 2019-20 and 2020-21 were impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic and are not directly comparable with past academic years.

## Insights
Student counts show the size of the HE ecosystem, while entrant counts indicate whether new
people are joining the learning system. If student numbers are falling, then this suggests that
the region’s overall learning base is shrinking. Falling entrants suggests that fewer new people
are stepping into the HE pathways which can be a warning sign for future workplace supply and
may present a strong link to LLL decline.
Tables 5 and 17 of the data referenced contains combined counts of Scottish-domiciled
students and new entrants in HE in both HEIs and colleges per LA, this data shows:
- Dundee retains a relatively healthy HE pipeline and good recruitment of new learners,
with students up +6.2% since 2013/14, and new entrants up +9.1% since 2013/14.
- Angus is losing both learners and new starters which are signs the LLL pipeline is
shrinking here, with students down -0.8% since 2013/14, and new entrants down -9.7%
since 2013-14 (with a notable -11.3% change from 2021-22).
- Fife shows an increase of 5.1% of students and a small increase of 1.4% of new entrants
since 2013/14.
- Perth & Kinross also shows an increase of 7.9% of students and a 4% increase in new
entrants since 2013/14.

## Conclusion
Colleges are primarily the entry point for adult learners, career-changers, and part-time learners as they offer shorter, flexible, and accessible learning opportunities, including work-based training and reskilling programmes. Colleges also often serve older learners, carers, low-income learners, minority students, and those from higher deprivation areas (The Scottish Government, 2018), yet since 2013-14, the Tay Cities region has experienced a significant shift in participation away from colleges towards universities.

HEI student numbers have grown – showing a 4,680 increase, yet college student numbers have declined – showing a 1,975 decrease since 2013-14. This increase has been driven mainly by The University of Dundee and The University of St Andrews, while Dundee & Angus College and Fife College have lost a significant proportion of their student base.

Across the Tay Cities learning institutions, the number of HEI qualifiers have risen since 2013-14 while college qualifiers have fallen. This has widened the completion gap and, because colleges disproportionately serve part-time, adult, and returning learners, this signals a clear decline in the region’s LLL provisions. Fewer qualifiers are coming through the FE side of the LLL ecosystem, which is a strong indicator of reduced LLL capacity.

The Scottish Government should prioritise a targeted reinvestment in college-level LLL pathways within the Tay Cities region including access programmes, short-cycle HE (HNC/HND), and adult-upskilling provision. A regional approach to rebalance provision, rebuild FE capacity, and increasing routes to HEIs could help widen equitable education. Strengthening colleges role in the learning pipeline could be essential to prevent further decline in LLL and to maintain inclusive skill growth across the region.

It is not possible to conclude from the small subset of data examined that the erosion of college student numbers is the direct result of funding withdrawal. However, The Scottish Government should consider reviewing the cumulative impact of recent funding withdrawal on adult-learning and reskilling pathways, especially those which support women, low-income adults, digital upskilling, and employer-driven development.

Therefore, while the evidence does not establish causation, it may suggest a credible risk that withdrawal of targeted adult-learning and reskilling funding has overall reduced access routes into FE within the Tay Cities region.
