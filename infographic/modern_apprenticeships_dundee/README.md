# Gender Distribution
To explore the gender balance for MA enrolments and visualise how Dundee’s gender distribution compares with other regions in Scotland, Table 2.8: Modern Apprenticeship starts by local authority area, Q2 2023/24 to Q2 2025/26 from the data was used.
Many of the ‘Female’ counts have been anonymised using secondary suppression (GSS, 2014) for data protection, so to get a general idea of the distribution of gender for as many regions as possible with the data available, I first calculated an estimate of the ‘Female’ counts based on the ‘Male’ counts and the ‘Total’ counts. I then combined the known ‘Prefer not to say/In another way’ counts with the ‘Female’ counts. There were either missing or anonymised data for Na h-Eileanan Siar, Orkney Islands, and areas Outwith the Scottish postcode area which appear blank in the visualisations.
This analysis revealed that for all Scottish regions:
- In Q2 2023/24, Edinburgh had the most equal gender distribution.
- In Q2 2024/25, Edinburgh, East Lothian, and Aberdeen had the most equal gender distribution.
- In Q2 2025/26, Aberdeen has close to an equal 50/50 gender distribution, with Edinburgh not far behind.

# Data Quality
There are many missing or removed data due to legal compliance with data protection laws. Many values have been anonymised using both primary and secondary suppression to ensure the protection of individuals. 
Skills Development Scotland state: “Disclosure control is applied to values less than five (marked with an asterisk *) or where such numbers can be identified by differencing.”
This is a data confidentiality and privacy measure to ensure that individuals cannot be identified. Many values for the ‘Female’ data have been suppressed using secondary suppression to protect the anonymity of values from the ‘Prefer not to say/In another way’ counts. This was likely done to avoid the possibility of calculating the – or differencing, the values by deriving them from the total counts.
For this reason, to perform gender analysis using data that had been suppressed, the ‘Female’ and ‘Prefer not to say/In another way’ categories were combined into one group as there was no way to distinguish the values independently due to the reasons mentioned above.


# Bias and Limitations
The gender distribution analysis concerns a real-world structural gender imbalance which is present in many areas, not just in the modern apprenticeship data. This analysis was done with the intent of understanding how this imbalance has changed over time to better understand possible ways to help balance this gap. Recognising the context of this bias is also important when interpreting variation in MA completion rate by gender which may also be influenced by sectoral composition rather than gender alone.
The data used contains some statistical bias as much of the gendered data was unavailable either due to anonymisation or it not existing. Instead, combined values were used to analyse the gender distribution of MAs, meaning some of the true values for ‘Female’ have been obscured and may vary in reality.
Skills Development Scotland also clearly included a disclaimer in their data stating they have made methodological changes for how they record data. They state that they have made these changes to accommodate a new system and improve compatibility with learning provider systems, along with streamlining data handling and analysis processes which may represent a measurement bias.
The accuracy of the data is both reliant on apprentices accurately self-reporting equality characteristics and is reliant on learning providers supplying data promptly and accurately. Many learning providers may have different frameworks to record information, which may lead to standardising issues when collecting the data.
